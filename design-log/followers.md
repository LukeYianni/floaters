# Followers — Design Log

## Inspiration sources

The follower system draws primarily from **Stonetop** (Jeremy Strandberg), with reference to **Blades in the Dark** (John Harper) for comparison.

Stonetop was read in full for this subsystem. Blades in the Dark was discussed from knowledge rather than direct reference.

---

## Tags vs. Edge/Flaw

**Considered:** Adopting Blades in the Dark's Edge/Flaw structure — one explicit strength, one always-on weakness — as the spine of the follower card.

**Decided:** Stick with Stonetop's tag system (2–4 descriptive adjectives/nouns) with a separate Instinct.

**Why:** The Edge/Flaw pairing forces the flaw to be the flip side of the strength — a savage gang is powerful *and* dangerous *because* of the same trait. That's elegant, but it doesn't feel true to how people work. A real person's weaknesses aren't always tied to their strengths. Keeping tags (capability) and instinct (automatic trouble) as separate elements feels more concrete and more human.

**Close call:** The BiTD structure is cleaner mechanically and would be easier to create followers with. The Stonetop approach requires more thought per follower. We accepted that tradeoff in favour of richer characterisation.

---

## Resilience track

**Considered:** Using the full Floaters wound system (Minor/Major/Critical slots with mechanical penalties per tier) for followers.

**Decided:** A single linear track of circles (2–4 depending on toughness). No per-hit penalties. Taken out when the last circle fills.

**Why:** The wound system's complexity is appropriate for PCs — it creates meaningful decisions about resistance, body parts, and recovery. For followers, that complexity is overhead. Followers should feel mortal and fragile without requiring the same bookkeeping. A linear track keeps the pressure without the mechanics.

**Reference point:** The Wild Sea was cited as a game that uses this style of linear circle track effectively.

---

## Instinct vs. Flaw

**Considered:** Whether Stonetop's instinct is just Floaters' version of BiTD's flaw.

**Decided:** Broadly yes, but with a meaningful difference: instinct is *triggered by fiction*, not always-on. A follower's instinct kicks in when scared, tempted, or without direction — not constantly. BiTD's flaw is structural and ever-present.

**Why it matters:** The triggered nature of instinct means it requires GM attention to activate. It won't cause trouble unless the situation creates the right conditions. This is a lighter touch that fits Floaters' fiction-first approach, but it also means the GM has to actively use instincts or they become inert flavour text.

**Note for future:** Worth monitoring in play whether instincts actually come up enough to matter, or whether they need to be made more structurally active.

---

## Edge interaction

**Considered:** Whether followers should interact with the Edge/Flow system at all, or sit outside it.

**Decided:** On a 6 on Order Followers, if the follower's action creates a clear opening, the directing PC gains 1 Edge. The PC can then convert that Edge to Flow by the normal rules (describe how it benefits the crew).

**Why:** Floaters' design principle is that risk generates Edge. Directing a follower into a dangerous situation is a real risk — to the follower, and to the operation. If that risk pays off, it should feed the economy. Keeping followers outside the Edge system would make them feel like tools divorced from the main engine.

**Close call on Flow:** There was genuine uncertainty about whether followers should be able to feed Flow. It feels slightly strange that a PC can coordinate a person they control to generate a crew resource. Decided it was acceptable for two reasons:
1. The fiction test is already built in — converting Edge to Flow requires describing a genuine crew benefit. If the opening doesn't actually help the crew, no Flow.
2. Forcing followers to be Flow-dead would create an awkward exception to an otherwise clean rule. The existing safeguards are sufficient.

**Key constraint:** Edge only generates from directed actions (Order Followers). A follower acting on their own instinct — without PC direction — never generates Edge. Edge comes from deliberate coordination, not from a follower going rogue.

---

## Loyalty structure

**Adopted directly from Stonetop** with no significant changes:
- Max 3 Loyalty
- Earned by paying the follower's cost (Strengthen Your Bond move)
- Spent 1-for-1 to override fear, instinct, or reluctance

The transactional feel of Loyalty was discussed briefly. Stonetop acknowledges this and says: for most followers, Loyalty represents trust, respect, fondness, or habit — not a ledger. The fiction of how the cost is paid and how Loyalty is spent should reflect that.

---

## What was not adopted from Stonetop

- **Damage dice** — Stonetop gives followers specific damage dice (1d6 hand, etc.). Floaters uses wounds as the damage system; the GM calls wound tier from fiction and position, same as any other consequence.
- **GM moves list** — Stonetop includes optional GM moves per follower. Not included for now; the instinct serves the same purpose with less overhead.
- **Group follower rules** — Stonetop has detailed rules for followers with the *group* tag (shared HP pools, individual members standing out, etc.). Not included in the initial draft. Could be added later if crew-scale followers become relevant to Floaters' setting.
