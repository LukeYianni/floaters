# Character Creation — Design Log

## Instinct

### What was decided

Step 1 (What Was Home?) now produces an Instinct — a named behavioral drive in the format:

> **NAME**
> To [infinitive phrase].

Players choose from a list of 12 examples or write their own in the same format. The Instinct feeds the end-of-session XP question: *"Did you demonstrate your Instinct, or struggle against it?"*

### Why Instincts replaced the Specialism XP question

The previous end-of-session question — *"Did you act on your Specialism in a way that created real risk?"* — was discarded because it was generic and rubberizable. Any player could claim it loosely. It pointed at capability, not character, and rewarded nothing specific.

Instinct points at a behavioral tendency rooted in who the character *was*, not what they can *do*. The end-of-session question rewards either leaning into it or resisting it — both directions create drama without prescribing which is correct.

### Why Instincts live in Step 1, not Step 3

The Specialism (Step 3) describes how a character operates — their method and expertise. The Instinct describes how their past shaped their behavior. Keeping them separate means two distinct XP sources with genuinely different textures: one fires in play when your method creates a consequence (Specialism trigger), one fires at end of session when your nature was present in the fiction (Instinct).

### What makes a good Instinct — the FREEDOM principle

When writing or evaluating an Instinct, the goal is a drive that surfaces naturally in play without announcing itself as a conflict trigger. FREEDOM (*"To chafe against rules, expectations, obligations"*) works better than DEFIANCE (*"To push back against what is demanded of you"*) because DEFIANCE telegraphs the tension — it tells the table when it's supposed to fire. FREEDOM just describes how this person is, and lets the fiction decide whether that drive creates friction or momentum in any given moment.

A good Instinct doesn't need to obviously create tension. It needs to be true to the character. The tension emerges from play.

A weak Instinct is one that only works in one direction, only fires in obvious situations, or reads more like a capability than a tendency.
