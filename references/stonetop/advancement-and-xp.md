# Stonetop — Advancement and XP

## How XP is Earned

XP is marked (one tick at a time) from three sources:

### 1. Rolling a 6-

Every time you roll a move and get a 6- (a miss), you mark XP — **unless the move explicitly says otherwise**. This is the default and most frequent source of XP. Some moves override it in both directions:

- Some 6- results say "don't mark XP" (e.g. Battle Joy, certain high-risk moves) — the consequence is punishment enough.
- A few moves say "mark XP" under specific other conditions.

This mechanic means failure is the primary driver of advancement. Acting and risking failure is the engine — hanging back produces nothing.

### 2. End of Session Move

At the end of every session, players run through a structured review. It has two layers:

**Personal questions (each player answers for themselves):**
- Did you demonstrate or struggle with your instinct? If yes, mark XP.
- Did your relationship with or opinion of a PC, NPC, or group change? If yes, mark XP.

**Group questions (answered collectively — everyone marks XP for each "yes"):**
- Did we learn more about the world or its history?
- Did we defeat a threat to Stonetop or the region?
- Did we improve our standing with our neighbours?
- Did we make a lasting improvement to Stonetop, or tangible progress towards doing so?

So in a full session, a player can mark up to 2 personal XP + up to 4 group XP = **6 XP maximum from the end of session move**, before any 6- triggers during play.

The end of session also includes:
- Praise something about the session (fiction or table).
- A wish for future sessions: "more __, less __, a chance to __."

### 3. Playbook-Specific Triggers

Many individual moves contain "mark XP" as part of their resolution. Examples from the playbooks:

- **The Blessed**: "Once per session, when your wild ways offend or alienate you from someone, mark XP."
- **The Would-be Hero (In Over Your Head)**: "When another PC rescues you from danger, mark XP."
- **The Marshal (Sir, Permission to Die, Sir)**: "If you let them go [after they survive], mark XP."
- **The Seeker (Never at a Loss)**: On a 6- for Know Things, you *may choose* not to mark XP — if you don't, the GM tells you something interesting anyway.

These are designed to reward playing into the character's specific fiction and identity, not just competence.

## The XP Scale

You start at **level 1** with **0 XP**.

To level up from your current level, you need XP equal to:

> **6 + (2 × current level)**

| Current Level | XP to Next Level | Running Total |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 8 | 8 |
| 2 | 10 | 18 |
| 3 | 12 | 30 |
| 4 | 14 | 44 |
| 5 | 16 | 60 |
| 6 | 18 | 78 |

There is no maximum level. Each level costs 2 more XP than the last — early levels come quickly, late levels are slow. When you level up, you subtract the threshold from your XP (you don't reset to zero — surplus carries over).

## Burn Brightly (Spending XP Early)

Once you have enough XP to level up (i.e. you've hit the threshold), you unlock **Burn Brightly**:

> Spend 2 XP after any roll to add +1 to that roll (maximum +1 per roll).

This lets players bleed off XP before formally leveling up. It rewards players who accumulate XP but haven't had a quiet moment to level. It's also a deliberate choice: spend XP now for a tactical bonus, or save it and level up sooner.

## Leveling Up

To level up, you need **a quiet stretch of time at home** — you cannot level mid-mission. When you do:

1. Subtract (6 + 2× current level) from your XP.
2. Increase your level by 1.
3. **Choose one new move** from your playbook, or from an insert class you've unlocked.
4. Class-specific steps: e.g., even-numbered levels give the Blessed +1 max Stock; the Lightbearer chooses a new Invocation.
5. Review your Instinct and Appearance — update anything that no longer fits.

**One move per level. Always.**

## What Moves Give You

Each playbook has a full move list. At character creation you start with 3–4 moves. Every level-up gives you one more.

Moves are gated by level:

- **No gate** — available from level 1
- **Requires level 2+** — early advancement tier
- **Requires level 6+** — the most powerful tier; only accessible in late play
- **Requires [specific move]** — chained prerequisites

Common advancement moves available to most playbooks (chosen on level up):

- **Improved Stat** — increase one stat by 1 (max +2). Can be taken multiple times.
- **Superior Stat** (requires level 6+) — increase one stat by +1 (max +3). Supersedes Improved Stat's cap.

**Stats do not improve automatically with levels.** The only way to raise a stat is to choose Improved Stat or Superior Stat as your level-up move. HP does not increase per level either.

Cross-playbook moves exist on some playbooks (Dabbler, Seasoned Warrior, Arts of War, Worldly, Versatile) — they let you pick a move from another playbook each time you take them, as long as you otherwise qualify.

## Key Design Observations

**Failure drives advancement.** The 6- trigger means players who act and fail regularly advance faster than cautious players who avoid rolls. This actively rewards risk-taking.

**End of session is split between character and crew.** Personal questions (instinct, relationships) and group questions (mission outcomes) fire separately. A player can be rewarded for character work even if the mission failed, and vice versa.

**Leveling is downtime-gated.** You need a quiet moment at home to level up. This prevents mid-mission power spikes and ties advancement to the fiction.

**Level 6 is a qualitative gate.** The most powerful moves are locked behind it. Characters below level 6 have real breadth; characters at 6+ unlock a different tier of capability.

**Burn Brightly creates a decision.** Once you're over the threshold, you can spend XP for immediate bonuses. This gives players agency over the timing of their advancement.

**New characters start at level 1 regardless of group level.** No catching up, no power-leveling. A level 1 character in a level 5 group is described as "still plenty effective" — the range of options is narrower, not the effectiveness.
