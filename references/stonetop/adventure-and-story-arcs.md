# Stonetop: Adventure & Story Arc Design

Sources: GM Playbook (all sections), Book I (pages 7–29, chapters Getting Started, First Adventure, Threats)

---

## The Core Concept

Stonetop has no pre-written adventure modules. Story arcs emerge from **Threats** — living antagonist forces that advance on their own if the PCs don't intervene. Adventures arise as responses to threats or opportunities. The GM prepares pressure, not plot.

---

## Campaign Flow of Play

A Stonetop campaign cycles through these phases (from the GM Playbook "Flow of Play" diagram):

1. **First Adventure** — session 1 hook; establish characters, set scene at home, drop a threat or opportunity, see how players react
2. **Expedition** — the PCs travel somewhere to deal with a threat/opportunity; legs of travel, challenges, surprises, resolution
3. **Aftermath or Crisis** (choice point):
   - *Aftermath* — PCs return home; determine what happened while they were gone; transition to downtime
   - *Crisis* — trouble erupts at home while PCs are away or they head out in response; possibly Meet With Disaster
4. **Downtime** — zoom out; deal with logistics; establish goals/intentions; play out key situations; show time passing; eventually trigger Seasons Change
5. **Seasons Change** — a formal time skip; zoom out further; advance all threats; establish new situation
6. Loop back to Expedition or handle a new Crisis

## Threats: The Engine of Story Arcs

Threats are the primary structural unit of Stonetop's campaign design. They represent lingering problems that cause trouble for PCs, the steading, or the wider region.

### When to Write Threats
- After the first session, based on what players revealed about their characters
- Between sessions when: Seasons Change creates a new threat, an NPC or thing introduced might cause trouble, or PCs are likely to encounter a threat soon

### Threat Format

Each threat has:
1. **Name and Type** — one of: Affliction, Beast, Institution, MacGuffin, Magical Entity, Rabble, Villain, Wildcard
2. **Distance** — Homefront, Nearby, or Distant (tracked on maps in the GM Playbook)
3. **Instinct** — what it naturally does; written as "to ___" (e.g., "to enrich himself")
4. **Description** — quick overview including related threats or NPCs
5. **Impending Doom** — the worst thing that happens if the threat goes unchecked; written as a single bad outcome with 1–4 escalating events (grim portents) leading to it
6. **Grim Portents** — 2–4 checkboxes representing escalating stages toward doom; each is a concrete, observable event
7. **Stakes Questions** — open questions about how the threat will develop (optional)
8. **GM Moves** — 2–4 specific moves (optional), plus custom player moves (optional)

### Threat Types and Their Moves

Each type has a characteristic move list:
- **Affliction** — spreads, mutates, drives people to desperation, sows panic
- **Beast** — stalks, builds a lair, flees or rages, grows in numbers
- **Institution** — sways opinion, acquires resources, changes laws, sends agents
- **MacGuffin** — reveals secrets, generates envy/discord, goes missing, fails at worst moment
- **Magical Entity** — spies from afar, appears in dreams, demands oaths, sends minions
- **Rabble** — grows in numbers, claims territory, undergoes leadership change, overwhelms
- **Villain** — grasps for power, finds weaknesses, makes offers, attacks ruthlessly
- **Wildcard** — acts on instinct, shows worth or lack, reveals secrets, bears witness

### Updating Threats Between Sessions
- Cross off threats that are no longer active
- Mark grim portents that occurred
- If the PCs foiled or changed a threat's course, update its grim portents and impending doom
- Move threats closer or farther on the tracker as appropriate

## Expeditions: The Adventure Unit

An expedition is the primary "adventure" structure — a trip somewhere for a purpose.

### Structure
1. **Chart a Course** — name the destination; GM states requirements, challenges, risks, time needed
2. **Legs of Travel** — for each leg: if familiar and short, gloss over; if not, describe and make GM moves. Ask "What do you do?" Resolve. Repeat.
3. **Points of Interest** — landmarks, places to frame scenes (near = tension, already there = scene)
4. **Making Camp** — how do you avoid attention, keep warm, make fire?
5. **Resolution at Destination** — play out the site

### Travel Challenges (soft GM moves during legs)
- Present a challenge from Chart a Course
- Present some other encounter
- Have an NPC/follower get into/cause trouble
- Stir up conflict between PCs
- Offer an opportunity as they travel

## Sites: Where Adventures Happen

A site is a specific location with its own story. Built in four steps:

1. **Foundation** — context, established details, purpose; build on what the players already know; exploit setting tables
2. **Story** — connections to PCs, prior events, open questions; answer questions to make decisions; create a timeline if needed
3. **Contents** — populate with NPCs, monsters, areas/rooms, dangers, discoveries; arrange areas in a nodal diagram or representational map
4. **Write it Up** — maps, visuals, detail rooms/areas with descriptions/impressions/exits; make lists and tables; plan GM moves, impending dooms, grim portents

### What Sites Contain
- **Discoveries** — clues, encounters, opportunities, artifacts & arcana
- **Hazards** — described as detailed descriptions, GM moves, impending dooms, or player moves
- **Encounters** — who/what, when/where, what they're doing, what they want, how they react to PCs, why they're in the game

---

## The Homefront: Between Adventures

The village isn't just backdrop — it has its own seasonal life and active Homefront threats. Between expeditions:

- Life continues: seasonal tasks (planting, harvest, distilling, hunting), NPC relationships, community needs
- Downtime actions: take care of logistics, establish goals, frame scenes, play out PC moves, describe time passing
- Aftermath scenes: what happened while PCs were gone?; return/post-crisis scenes; family/NPC scenes

---

## Key Design Principles of Stonetop Arcs

1. **Threats, not plots** — the GM prepares antagonist forces with instincts and doom; the plot is what happens when PCs collide with those forces
2. **Distance as pacing** — Homefront threats are immediate; Nearby threats are upcoming; Distant threats are slow-burn; threats move closer over time
3. **Grim portents as clock** — each portent is a concrete, visible step toward doom; PCs see the clock ticking in fiction
4. **Expeditions as reactive** — adventures are usually responses to threats or opportunities, not pre-planned modules
5. **Session 0 generates threats** — what players reveal about their characters (backstories, special possessions, relationships) directly seeds what threats the GM writes
6. **Seasons Change as pacing valve** — formally advances all threats; marks time; resets the situation for the next cycle
