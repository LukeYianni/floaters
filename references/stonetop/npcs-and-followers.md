# Stonetop: NPCs & Followers

Summary of how Stonetop designs, portrays, and statlines NPCs and Followers. Use this as a reference when designing Floaters equivalents — don't re-read the PDF.

---

## What NPCs Are

NPCs are anyone in the game world who isn't a PC. This includes people, spirits, beasts, and bound entities — anything with a personality the PCs can interact with.

NPCs can also be:
- **Followers** — NPCs who take orders from a PC
- **Monsters** — NPCs the PCs can fight (see `monsters-and-dangers.md`)
- **Threats** — NPCs causing ongoing trouble (see `adventure-and-story-arcs.md`)

The same NPC can shift between these roles over time.

---

## Creating NPCs

Build only what you'll actually use. **Prep only as much as you find useful and valuable.**

### Step 1: Name
Give the NPC a name or ask a player to. Names should feel regionally appropriate.

### Step 2: Concept
Answer: What are the most obvious things about them? Pronouns, age, origin. What would you call them in three words?

**Build on what's established** — extrapolate from what players revealed. Ask the players questions about NPCs they'd know. The more the PCs know an NPC, the more questions you should ask.

**For locals or people PCs know well:**
- Are you related to them? How?
- What's their family situation?
- Who else are they close to?
- What do you like/dislike about them?
- What are they respected for?
- What do others say behind their back?
- What's their most notable feature?
- How have they always treated you?

**For outsiders the PCs know:**
- When/how did you first meet?
- What do you know of their family?
- Why do you (not) look forward to seeing them again?
- How have they changed since you last met?

**For NPCs the PCs have heard of but never met:**
- What are they known for?
- What's said to be their most notable trait?
- Who do you know who's met them?

### Step 3: Impressions (up to 3)
Short, evocative sensory details. Should show rather than tell. At least one should contrast with the rest of their concept.

Examples by category:
- **Face:** angular, broken nose, dimpled, hawk nose, leathery, missing teeth, scar, tattoo, etc.
- **Eyes:** big, bright, cloudy, dark, droopy, squinty, watery, etc.
- **Body:** big, heavy, little, lithe, meaty, round, stooped, thick, thin, wiry, etc.
- **Presence:** alert, brooding, cheery, fidgety, friendly, haughty, hunched, intense, serene, etc.
- **Scent:** earthy, musky, floral, ripe, sour, smoky, etc.
- **Voice:** breathy, clipped, crass, gruff, hoarse, lilting, monotone, mumbly, rumbling, shrill, stutter, etc.

### Step 4: Additional Details (as needed)

**Assign a memorable trait** — one specific, memorable word or phrase. This is your touchstone for portraying them. Folks won't remember names but they'll remember "the girl who runs everything."

**Instinct** — what they naturally do, written as "to [verb]." Write this between sessions.
- If they're a monster or threat: instinct should bring them into conflict with others
- If they're a follower: instinct should cause trouble for the PC who leads them
- Anyone else: instinct reflects their basic outlook and approach to the world

**Connections to others:**
- What do they think of the PCs?
- Who are they related to? Friends with?
- Who are they loyal to, and why?
- Who do they dislike, and why?

**Motivations:**
- What do they want from the PCs?
- What do they fear?
- What angers them?
- What do they long for?
- What do they think they deserve?
- What do they aspire to do or be?

**Embodiment** — pick one or more tricks for portraying this NPC:
- Pick an actor or character from TV/film/theater. Try to portray them.
- Pick someone you know personally. Impersonate them, but don't tell anyone.
- Pick a way of speaking, a catch phrase, a physical tic, or a behavior. Use it whenever you portray them.
- Find or create a picture of them.

**Game stats** — assign only if needed:
- If they might fight the PCs: stat them as a **monster**
- If they'll cause ongoing trouble: stat them as a **threat**
- If they'll regularly act on a PC's orders: stat them as a **follower**
- If none of the above: use follower guidelines anyway, or just wing it

## Portraying NPCs at the Table

The GM decides how NPCs react, what they say, and what happens to them. **Portray NPCs as part of the core loop** — establish situations, make moves with them, say what they do.

### Modes of portrayal

**First-person (speaking in voice):** "I… just don't know if I can keep doing this." More immersive; prompts players to respond in kind. Fun but requires skill.

**Third-person (summarizing):** "She tears up and says she can't keep doing this." Easier; less acting required; more intentional control over what you share.

**In practice, switch between modes** — start third-person, deliver a key line in first person. Summarize a long conversation, punctuate with a few voice lines.

### Dialogue scenes
Good dialogue scenes have a purpose: there's something the PCs want to accomplish or a question that needs answering. Keep the core loop running. Establish the situation, make a soft GM move, ask "What do you do?"

When the scene's purpose is resolved or the dialogue starts to fizzle, **wrap the scene**. Step out of character, move to the next scene.

### Multiple NPCs at once
Balance giving NPCs life with being a fan of the PCs. Keep conversation centered on the PCs. Have NPCs appeal to them and get them to take sides. Use third-person more than first-person when NPCs are in conflict.

## Persuading NPCs

**PERSUADE (vs. NPCs):** When you *press or entice an NPC*, say what you want them to do (or not do). If they have reason to resist, roll +CHA:
- **10+:** They either do as you want or reveal the easiest way to convince them
- **7–9:** They reveal something you can do to convince them, though it'll likely be costly, tricky, or distasteful

Things that might convince an NPC:
- A promise/oath/vow
- A chance to do it safely, freely, or discreetly
- Appealing to their ego, honor, conscience, or fears
- A better or excessive offer
- Helping them or doing it with them
- Violence (or a credible threat of it)
- Something they want or need (coin, food, booze, etc.)
- Assurance, proof, or corroboration
- Pressure or permission from someone they respect

## Followers

Followers are NPCs who generally take orders from a PC and follow their lead. They are different from other NPCs in three ways:
1. They'll generally do what their PC says
2. They can accumulate **Loyalty** (max 3), which makes them easier to lead
3. When a PC directs them to do something, they can trigger **player moves**

### Follower Stats

Every follower has:
- **Tags** — 2–4 adjectives or nouns describing what they are and do
- **HP** — how resilient they are
- **Armor** — how well-protected
- **Damage** — what they deal
- **Instinct** — what they naturally do that causes trouble for their PC
- **Cost** — what keeps them following (paid to generate Loyalty)
- **Loyalty** — a currency (0–3) the PC spends to override fear or reluctance

### Creating Followers (Step by Step)

**Step 1: Basics** — Flesh them out as an NPC. Name, concept, how you'll describe them. What distinguishes them from other NPCs? Why do they follow the PCs?

**Step 2: Tags** — Give 2–4 tags, sometimes more. Tags are adjectives or nouns finishing: "This follower is/is a ___." Tags guide portrayal AND affect rolls when players Order Followers.

Useful tags: *agile, archer, athletic, beautiful, brave, cunning, fast, fierce, hardy, healer, intimidating, magical, observant, organized, patient, respected, self-sufficient, sharp-eyed, stealthy, tireless, tracker, warrior, ___-wise*

Problematic tags: *bigoted, drunk, greedy, gullible, lecherous, naive, proud, rookie, reckless, short-fused, stubborn, frail*

Mixed-blessing tags: *animal-lover, annoying, big, bully, callous, cautious, devious, eager, thieving, gossipy, honest, kind, little, shameless, terrifying*

Reserve the **exceptional** tag for truly outstanding followers. They get +2 on rolls (instead of +1) when they have a relevant tag.

**Step 3: Hit Points**

| Resilience | HP |
|---|---|
| Weak/frail/soft | 3 |
| Able-bodied | 6 |
| Tough/strong/hard | 9 |

Modifiers: *tiny* −2 HP; *large* +4 HP; fates smile on them +2 HP

**Step 4: Armor**

| Protection | Armor |
|---|---|
| Nothing but cloth and flesh | 0 |
| Leathers or thick hide | 1 |
| Mail, scales, or similar | 2 |
| Steel, bony plates, carapace | 3 |
| Potent magical wards | 4 |

Modifiers: shield or similar +1; skilled in defense +1; lacks vital organs +1; *tiny* +1

**Step 5: Damage**

| Danger | Damage |
|---|---|
| Not very | d4 |
| Can defend themselves | d6 |
| Veteran fighter/predator | d8 |

Range and other tags are based on gear. For monstrous followers, use monster damage guidelines.

**Step 6: Instinct** — What they naturally do that causes trouble for the PC who leads them. Written as "to [verb]."

**Step 7: Moves (optional)** — Write up to 3 GM moves, reflecting abilities not covered by a tag, how they use a specific tag, or common behaviors.

**Step 8: Cost** — What keeps them following. Usually a few words: "coin, payment, treasure" or "affection, respect" or "training." When a PC pays the follower's cost, the follower holds +1 Loyalty (max 3).

**Step 9: Equipment** — Decide what gear the follower carries, or have the player Outfit them.

### Loyalty

- Followers hold up to 3 Loyalty
- Paying a follower's cost generates +1 Loyalty
- Loyalty is spent 1-for-1 to have followers:
  - Overcome their fear to do as you say
  - Resist acting on their instinct/tags/traits
  - Do something they don't want to do (as long as it's not abhorrent or suicidal)
- When a follower is without orders or acts on their own initiative, the GM says what they do

### Ordering Followers (Move)

When you **direct your follower to do something that would trigger a player move**, and they do it, they trigger the move. Roll for them instead. Roll +STAT, then:
- If they have at least one appropriate tag or move: add +1 (or +2 if *exceptional*)
- If they have no relevant tag or move: add +0
- If any of their tags would get in the way: roll with disadvantage

### Followers Resist Orders When...
- They're angry, miserable, shocked, or overwhelmed
- The order is unreasonable, foolish, degrading, distasteful, or goes against their instinct, tags, cost, or other traits

When a follower resists, reveal this to the player. The player can spend Loyalty to have them comply, Persuade them, or let it go.

### Group Followers

A follower with the *group* tag represents multiple individuals sharing stats (tags, HP, armor, damage, instinct, moves, cost). They act as one.

- Hold a common pool of Loyalty (spendable on the whole group or individual members)
- Each member has their own HP
- When a group **Clashes or Lets Fly:** attack can hurt multiple foes; roll once, roll damage separately against each foe
- When a group **Defends:** holds a common pool of Readiness
- When a group **Recovers:** each member who regains HP must consume 1 use of Supplies
- When a group **Seeks Insight:** PC can ask 3 questions total (not per member)
- When individual members **stand out:** give them a name, a memorable trait, maybe an extra tag

### Followers at 0 HP

When a follower reaches 0 HP, their fate is in the hands of the source of damage:
- **If potentially lethal:** they're dead immediately, trigger Death's Door, or they're dying (will die or roll Death's Door if no one intervenes)
- **If not lethal:** they're out of action until you say otherwise, or they recover HP via rest

**Remember:** It's not your job to protect your NPCs and followers. **Let things burn.**

### Converting Between Follower and Monster

**Monster → Follower:** Add any appropriate tags; choose (or make up) a cost; add a spot to track Loyalty (max 3). Otherwise use stats as-is.

**Follower → Monster:** Use stats as-is. If no GM moves exist, write some now.

## Key Principles

- **Give your characters life** — portray NPCs as distinct, memorable, believable people
- **Be a fan of the player characters** — NPCs should create drama that's engaging for the PCs, not drama that sidelines them
- **Play to find out what happens** — let NPC motivations drive behavior; don't predetermine outcomes
- **Let things burn** — don't protect your NPCs; if the fiction and dice point toward harm, follow through
