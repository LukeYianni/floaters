# Stonetop: Encounters & Sites

Summary of how Stonetop designs locations, encounters, hazards, and discoveries. Use this as a reference when designing Floaters equivalents — don't re-read the PDF.

---

## Sites

A **site** is a specific location with its own story. Adventures happen at sites. Interesting sites:
- Tell a story via their environment
- Are exciting to explore
- Present players with interesting decisions

Every area/room/scene in a site should have **at least one detail or element that reflects the site's story.**

### Building a Site (4 Steps)

**Step 1: Lay the Foundation**
- Ask questions about what the PCs would know about this place
- Build on what you've already established (context, established details, purpose)
- Exploit the setting guide — use relevant tables, procedures, examples

**Step 2: Build Up the Story**
- Look for connections to setting elements: the PCs, things they care about, threats, NPCs, past events, open questions
- Ask yourself questions that arise naturally from all of the above
- Answer those questions; make decisions. Look for new connections as you do.
- Create a timeline of the site's story; clarify and revise as needed

**Step 3: Sketch Out the Contents**
- Populate with NPCs and monsters appropriate to the foundation and story
- Identify dangers and discoveries implied by the foundation and story
- Establish areas/rooms suggested by the foundation, denizens, and common sense
- Arrange areas in a **nodal diagram** or representational map — write down connections between them

**Step 4: Write It Up**
- Create maps or visuals if helpful
- Detail areas/rooms: descriptions, impressions, questions, content, story, exits
- Find/create content: dangers, discoveries, NPCs; pick from Book II or make them up
- Make lists and tables to pick/roll on
- Make plans: GM moves, if/thens, Die of Fate tables, custom moves, timetables, impending dooms & grim portents
- Review and revise. Fix inconsistencies.

### Site Considerations

When exploring a site, keep in mind:
- **Physical space:** dimensions, construction, clearance, footing, levels, etc.
- **Light sources:** who has them, range/tags, who out there sees them, duration
- **Senses beyond sight:** sounds, odors, textures, heat/cold, humidity, feelings
- **Marching order:** especially in tight spots
- **Followers & NPCs:** where they are, what they're doing, how they react
- **Physical and mental states:** hunger, thirst, fatigue, irritability, confusion, dread
- **Denizens:** signs of their presence, how they react to PCs

### Exploration

When PCs move on, identify the next interesting scene/situation in the site. Establish the PC's intent. Compare to your prep or sense of the site. If the next scene/situation is obvious, great.

If not, you might:
- Rely on a player move (Defy Danger to follow directions without getting lost, Seek Insight to see what they find, etc.)
- Make a GM move, frame the action where it'd make sense to make that move
- Pick/roll on a relevant table or use an example site from Book II

Then frame the action at the next scene/situation, and go from there.

## Encounters

Encounters are who or what the PCs run into at a site. When designing or running an encounter, know:

1. **Who/what is the encounter with?**
2. **When/where does it happen?**
3. **What are they doing?**
4. **Why are they here?**
5. **What do they want?**
6. **How do they react to the PCs?**
7. **Why are you putting this encounter in the game? What's at stake?**

Encounters might be discovered as **clues** (pointing toward the encounter), as **direct confrontation**, or as **opportunities** (a chance to engage with them on favorable terms).

### Framing Encounters

Introduce encounters via GM moves — frame a scene, put the thing in it, ask "What do you do?"

**If you want to build tension:** frame the scene with the location in sight but at a distance. Otherwise, frame the scene with them already there.

**If the location is unfamiliar:** describe it, give impressions. If the PCs know or picked this place, ask them instead:
- What's the most striking thing you notice?
- What here tells you this is a place where/of/that ___?
- What have you heard about this place?
- When were you here last? How has it changed?
- How does this place make you feel?
- What are you thinking/worrying about?

Portray NPCs, add details, answer questions. Make a soft GM move, then ask "What do you do?"

## Discoveries

Discoveries are things the PCs find at a site. Four types:

### Clues
Start with a revelation, the thing the PCs can learn. What could lead to that revelation?

Sources of clues:
- Signs left in the environment (tracks, etc.)
- Physical remains (blood, bone, scat, etc.)
- Something out of place
- Writing, art, markings (a note, graffiti, etc.)
- An NPC's or monster's behavior
- Noises, glimpses, scents
- Rumors, reports, tales, gossip
- Flashes, omens, revelations

### Encounters
(See section above.)

### Opportunities
- Useful or valuable stuff
- Expendable resources
- A good spot to make camp, set a trap, etc.
- The means to get somewhere
- A chance to ___
- A novel/pleasant experience
- A resource the PCs might come back for
- A place of power
- An arcanum
- Anything else that comes to mind

### Artifacts & Arcana
Place arcana where they make sense and where they tell a story. Be generous with info.

If they get a 7+ to Know Things, reveal some combo of: what it is/does, what it's worth, how they might activate it, or sell it for full value, or how they might learn more.

- **On a 7–9:** reveal the trigger of an artifact's move or the front of an arcanum
- **On a 10+:** give them the full move or both front and back of an arcanum

If they can't figure it out, give them a path: tell them the requirements, Make a Plan, write a love letter, or just tell them after some downtime.

## Hazards

**Hazards** are environmental dangers. They can't be fought — only avoided, endured, or overcome. The GM introduces them as fiction and makes moves with them.

### Presenting a Hazard

**As a detailed description:** Just describe what it is, what it looks like, what it does, how it works.

**As GM moves:** Write one or more GM moves that reflect:
- How its presence is foreshadowed or revealed
- How it harms or hinders
- How it escalates or gets worse
- How it thwarts attempts to overcome it

**If dynamic or changing:** give it an instinct, written as "to ___" (e.g., "to bury everything").

**As an impending doom:** Write the ultimate bad thing that can happen (e.g., "tunnel collapses; they roll Death's Door"). Write 1–4 events describing how it starts and escalates; assign each event one or more checkboxes. Optionally write a trigger (fictional or mechanical).

**As a player move:** Write a fictional trigger ("When you *trigger the hazard*, ...") and resolution, using any of the following:
- ___ happens (and ___ is bad)
- Pick X from a list
- Tell us ___
- Lose ___
- Take damage/suffer a debility/Death's Door
- Roll +STAT (for something); on a 10+, ___; on a 7–9, ___ (optionally); on a 6-, ___

### Hazard Damage

**What would it do to a typical person?** (pick 1)
| Severity | Damage |
|---|---|
| Bruises & scrapes; pain; light burns | d4 |
| Nasty flesh wounds, bruises, burns | d6 |
| Broken bones; deep/wide burns | d8 |
| Death or dismemberment | d10 |

Additional modifiers (all that apply):
- Armor can't protect them: *ignores armor*
- Cuts through leather/hide: 1 piercing, *messy*
- Tears metal apart: 3 piercing, *messy*
- Knocks them around: *forceful*
- Big/vicious/scary: +2 damage
- They've taken precautions: +disadvantage
- They're caught off-guard: +advantage

## GM Moves (General)

During play, the GM makes **soft moves** (provoke action, increase tension) and **hard moves** (establish badness after a 6- or ignoring trouble). GM moves include:

**General moves:**
- Announce trouble (future or offscreen)
- Reveal an unwelcome truth
- Ask a provocative question
- Put someone in a spot
- Use up their resources
- Turn their move back on them
- Demonstrate a downside
- Hurt someone
- Separate them
- Capture someone
- Offer an opportunity (with or without a cost)
- Tell them the consequences/requirements
- Advance towards impending doom

**Exploration moves:**
- Provide a choice of paths
- Hint at more than meets the eye
- Offer riches at a price
- Present a discovery
- Point to a looming danger
- Introduce a danger, person, or faction
- Bar the way

**Homefront moves:**
- Introduce someone interesting
- Reveal simmering tensions
- Present a want or need
- Show how others really feel
- Draw out their feelings
- Change a relationship
- Oppose their wishes
- Remind them of their obligations
- Start a conflict or crisis
- Play them against each other

## The Core Loop (for Reference)

1. **Establish the situation:** frame the action, describe the environment, give details and specifics, ask questions for input, portray NPCs and monsters, answer questions and clarify
2. **Make a soft GM move:** provoke action and/or increase tension
3. **Ask "What do you do?"**
4. **Resolve their actions:**
   - If they trigger a player move, do what the move says
   - If they roll a 6-, make a hard GM move
   - If they ignore trouble, make a hard GM move
   - Otherwise, say what happens
5. **Repeat**

## Key Design Principles

- **Prep pressure, not plot** — prepare antagonist forces with instincts, prepare sites with contents; the adventure is what happens when PCs collide with those
- **Every scene needs a purpose** — frame scenes around choices, not just description
- **Let things burn** — don't protect your prepared content from the dice; if the fiction points toward harm or destruction, follow through
- **Begin and end with the fiction** — every mechanical trigger requires a fictional justification; every outcome should be expressed as fiction first
- **Hint at more than meets the eye** — before fights or encounters, give PCs a chance to assess, prepare, or flee
