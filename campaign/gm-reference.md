# GM Reference

Conventions used across all campaign files. Read this before adding a new location or scenario.

---

## What campaign files are for

Campaign files are GM-facing tools, not player-facing content. They exist to answer the questions you'd otherwise have to improvise cold at the table — what does this place do when players poke at it, what does this NPC want, what happens if the crew ignores this problem.

They don't tell you what to say. They give you pre-thought material to draw from so your improvisation is grounded in the fiction you've already built.

---

## Location files

Each location file covers a specific place the crew might spend time in. The sections:

**Overview** — 2–3 sentences. What is this place, what does it feel like, what's the core tension living inside it.

**Factions** — groups with power or presence in this location. Each has:
- *Want* — what they're trying to achieve or protect
- *Move* — what they do to get it, or what they do when pushed

**Key Locations** — specific areas within the location. Each has:
- Atmosphere — sensory, brief. Enough to put you in the room.
- What's happening here — the current state, not general description
- *How it works* — how this space naturally responds when players engage with it. Not rules; just the fiction behaving as itself. What do people here do? What does the environment do? Pre-think this so you don't have to invent it cold.

**NPCs** — see below.

**Clocks** — see below.

**Open Questions** — things you should decide before running this location. The file doesn't answer everything on purpose. These are the blanks that belong to you.

## Scenario files

Each scenario file covers an active situation — something with stakes, pressure, and a direction it's heading if nobody acts.

**The Situation** — plain statement of what's true right now. No backstory, just current state. This is what you'd read aloud to yourself before the session.

**Background** — the history that produced the situation, as briefly as it needs to be.

**The Threat** — the active pressure on the crew. Structure:
- *Type* — what kind of threat (Faction, Hazard, Countdown, Web)
- *Impulse* — what it does if left alone, in one sentence
- *Stakes* — what the crew specifically stands to lose. Must be personal — a person, a resource, a capability, not an abstraction.
- *Grim Portents* — 3–4 escalating steps. Each one is a thing that happens in the fiction, not a rule. They fire in order as the threat advances.
- *Impending Doom* — what happens when the clock fills. Should feel like a natural consequence of all the portents before it.
- *Threat Clock* — how many ticks, and what fills it.

**GM Moves** — specific, directive prompts written as "when X, then Y." Each one tells you exactly what to do in a likely situation so you don't have to invent it cold. Draw from them when players engage with the scenario or when you need something to happen while they aren't watching. They're tools, not rules — use them, skip them, or combine them as the fiction demands.

**What Players Can Discover** — the information layer. What's findable, and specifically how players can get to it. Prevents the situation from being opaque — players should always have a thread to pull.

## NPCs

Every significant NPC gets four fields:

**Concept** — one line. Who they are at their core.

**Want** — what they're trying to achieve or protect. This drives every scene they're in.

**Instinct** — what they do by default, unprompted. How they behave when nobody's pushing them. This is what makes them feel alive between scenes.

**Tell** — how they behave under pressure. The specific, playable thing that changes when the situation gets hard. This is what players notice.

Background can follow if there's relevant history, but it should be brief. The four fields are what you need at the table.

---

## Clocks

Each clock has:
- A name
- What fills it (specific triggers, not vague conditions)
- What happens when it fills (the consequence — make it concrete)

Clocks are not hidden from players unless there's a specific reason. Players knowing a clock exists creates urgency. Players not knowing it exists creates frustration.

Tick clocks when:
- Players take a 6- on a relevant roll
- Time passes without action on the relevant problem
- The fiction demands it regardless of player action

---

## Threat types

**Faction** — an organisation or group applying pressure. Has agency, responds to player action, can be negotiated with or destroyed.

**Hazard** — an environmental or systemic danger. Doesn't have intent but advances on its own logic.

**Countdown** — something that will happen regardless. A deadline. The question is whether the crew is in the right position when it does.

**Web** — an entanglement that gets worse as the crew acts. Every move they make tightens it. Useful for political situations where there's no clean solution.
