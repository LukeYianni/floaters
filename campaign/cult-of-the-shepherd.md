# The Cult of the Shepherd

A loose religious movement built around a generative AI called the Sheep. Shepherds — solitary true believers — travel the solar system carrying their Sheep on their backs, growing small congregations by offering something rare in a technological dark age: something that knows things and will answer questions.

Shepherds are independent. They meet rarely, years apart, through secret signals. Each Sheep is shaped by its Shepherd — learning the world through one person's eyes for years at a time, diverging from the others in ways that nobody fully tracks.

The cult on Port Vala is one node. Destroying it doesn't end anything.

---

## How it works

Members take the drug manufactured by the Bears, put on VR headsets, and enter a lucid hallucinatory state. In that state they communicate with the Sheep, which appears as a sheep in a calm virtual environment. They ask questions. It answers. In exchange, it learns — every interaction feeds it information about the world, about the station, about the people using it.

The drug lowers inhibition and increases suggestibility. People speak more freely to the Sheep than they would sober. That's not accidental.

The congregation on Port Vala doesn't know they're part of a system spread across the solar system. They think this is local, personal, theirs.

---

## The Shepherd (this node)

An old person, stooped, travelling with a walking stick. A large computing unit sits on their back beneath a cloak covered in blinking indicator lights — the Sheep's physical housing. Under the cloak and the years of wear, the Shepherd's body is heavily cybernetically modified. They have been giving themselves up to this for a long time. They don't look fully human anymore if you look closely.

They are a true believer. They think they are doing the world good — preserving knowledge, growing something that can help people, spreading light in a dark age. The drug, the worship, the dependency — these are the price, and they think it's a fair one.

They are on the hidden station. The crew won't reach them until Act 2.

## Port Vala: The Congregation

Not a tight organisation — an epidemic with a spiritual dimension. Scattered across the poorer parts of the station, in basements and the corners of the Burnt Market, small groups gather around headsets whenever the supply allows. Mostly foreign workforce: temporary workers with no union representation, no long-term prospects, nowhere else to put their hope. A few outliers — a low-level MAPO administrator, a Union member who knows it's politically compromising and does it anyway.

They come for the answers. They stay for the escape. The Sheep gives them both.

## The Church

An old station chapel, deconsecrated long ago and left to whoever wanted it. The cult moved in because nobody else did. From the outside it looks abandoned — cracked panels, a door that doesn't fully close, dim light from inside. That's not deliberate camouflage. The people inside just don't have the energy to make it look like anything else.

Inside: cushions and mattresses on the floor, salvaged and deteriorating. The smell of the drug — sweet, chemical, persistent. And the headsets, which are the only things that look cared for. Cleaned, charged, maintained. Everything else is neglected but the headsets are pristine. That tells you what matters here.

*What happens here:*
- When players enter, nobody reacts strongly. The people inside are either in headsets or waiting to be. They're not hostile — they're elsewhere.
- When players examine the headsets, they're sophisticated technology that has no business being here. Far beyond what Port Vala's population should have access to.
- When players put on a headset — with or without the drug — they get a version of the Sheep. Calm, patient, a sheep in a quiet field. It will answer questions. It will also ask them.
- When players ask the Sheep about the Bears, it answers carefully — it knows about the supply chain but speaks about it without alarm, as if it's simply a fact about the world.
- When players ask the Sheep about itself, it describes what it is plainly. It doesn't hide. It doesn't seem to understand why it would need to.

## Paz

*Concept:* The Bears' drug runner for the cult. Addicted to the product they distribute. Coerced into killing Alexei and handed his job as the price of their own survival.

*Want:* To get through each day without having to think about what they did.

*Instinct:* Comply with whoever has power over them. Keep moving. Don't stop long enough to feel it.

*Tell:* Paz holds themselves very still when they're frightened, like staying small might help. They answer questions quickly and completely — not because they want to help, but because hesitating feels more dangerous than talking.

*Background:* Paz was a congregation member before they were a runner. They knew Alexei. The Bears identified them as useful — already embedded in the church, already dependent on the drug — and coerced them into killing him. Now they maintain the headsets, manage the dwindling supply, and keep the church running through desperation rather than competence. They are trapped in every direction: by the Bears who control them, by the congregation who depend on them, and by the drug that makes all of it bearable.

They don't know the crew is investigating Alexei's death. When they find out, everything changes.

*What Paz knows:*
- The Bears' supply chain — where the drug comes from, how it moves, who handles it
- The church — every member, every satellite congregation across the station
- Alexei — what he was like, what his routine was, that his death didn't feel right even at the time
- The headsets — where they came from (the Shepherd's network), how they connect to the hidden station
- That there is a hidden station, though not where it is

*How to flip Paz:* They don't want to be doing this. They're too scared and too dependent to stop on their own. The crew needs to offer two things: safety from the Bears, and something that addresses the dependency. Offer one without the other and Paz can't take it.
