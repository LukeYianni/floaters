# Followers

## What Is a Follower

A follower is an NPC who takes orders from a specific PC and generally does what that PC says. Followers are people first — they have instincts, needs, and limits. What distinguishes them from other NPCs is that they accumulate **Loyalty**, which makes them easier to lead and manage, and when a PC directs them into action, they trigger player moves.

Followers are always the GM's responsibility to portray. The player directs them; the GM decides what they actually do, say, and feel.

---

## The Follower Card

```
NAME, brief descriptor
tag, tag, tag, tag

Resilience:  ○ ○ ○
Armor:       ○ (if relevant)
Instinct:    to [do something]
Cost:        [what they need]   Loyalty ○○○

Short description — who they are, how they act.
```

### Tags

Give each follower 2–4 tags: adjectives or nouns that complete the sentence "this follower is ___." Tags do two things simultaneously:

- **Fiction**: they describe how the follower acts and what they're capable of. Use them to decide what the follower will and won't do.
- **Mechanics**: when you Order Followers, tags modify the roll.

Tags can be useful (*reliable, marksman, fast*), problematic (*reckless, paranoid, green*), or mixed (*aggressive* — good in a fight, bad in a negotiation). Give followers a mix.

**Useful tags:** agile, calm, capable, connected, discreet, fast, fierce, loyal, perceptive, reliable, resourceful, steady, tough, trained

**Problematic tags:** cowardly, green, impulsive, paranoid, reckless, short-fused, slow, squeamish, unreliable

**Mixed tags:** aggressive, ambitious, independent, literal-minded, proud, talkative

### Resilience

Followers use a single linear track instead of the full wound system. When a follower takes harm, fill one circle. When the last circle fills, they are taken out — dead, incapacitated, or out of the fight. The GM narrates what each hit looks like based on the fiction.

| Follower type | Track |
|---|---|
| Ordinary person | ○ ○ |
| Capable / trained | ○ ○ ○ |
| Tough / exceptional | ○ ○ ○ ○ |

### Instinct

Write the follower's instinct as: **"to [do something]."**

The instinct is what the follower naturally does when not given clear direction, when they are scared, or when they are tempted. It is a source of ongoing trouble for the PC they follow. Use it to drive scenes and complicate situations.

Examples:
- *To push the situation further than asked*
- *To default to violence when things get tense*
- *To protect their own skin first*
- *To question authority before following orders*
- *To take things personally*

### Cost

A follower's cost is what keeps them following. It is usually a few words: *respect and fair pay*, *to be kept in the loop*, *a sense of purpose*, *recognition*. When the PC pays the cost, the follower holds Loyalty. When the PC consistently fails to pay it, the follower drifts — or leaves.

### Loyalty

Followers hold **Loyalty** up to a maximum of 3. Loyalty is a currency the PC can spend to override a follower's fear or instinct.

Spend 1 Loyalty to have a follower:
- Overcome their fear and act despite it
- Resist acting on their instinct
- Do something they would otherwise refuse (as long as it isn't abhorrent or suicidal)

## Follower Moves

These moves trigger when a PC interacts with their follower.

### Order Followers

When you **direct your follower to do something that would trigger a roll**, you roll instead of them. Choose the relevant action (Fight, Prowl, Sway, etc.) and build your pool normally.

**Modify the pool based on tags:**
- A tag that clearly helps: **+1d**
- A tag that clearly hinders: **roll with disadvantage** (two dice, take lowest)
- No relevant tag: roll as normal

| Roll | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 6 | They do it cleanly. If their action creates a clear opening for you or the crew, gain **1 Edge**. |
| 4–5 | They do it, but with a complication, cost, or partial result. |
| 1–3 | They freeze, misread the situation, or their instinct kicks in. The GM decides what happens. |

**On a 1–3**, the GM may also show a downside — the follower is in danger, they've made things worse, or their instinct has created a new problem.

Edge gained from a follower opening can be converted to Flow by the normal rules — describe how the opening benefits the whole crew.

### Strengthen Your Bond

When you **pay your follower's cost** and haven't done so recently, they hold **+1 Loyalty** (max 3).

Paying a cost should be a meaningful fictional moment — not a transaction, but an act of attention. The PC claps them on the shoulder, gives them what they need, makes them feel seen. Work with the player to establish what paying this particular cost actually looks like.

## Followers in Play

### Directed vs. Acting on Instinct

**Order Followers triggers only when the PC directs the follower.** If a follower acts on their own instinct — without instruction — the GM decides what they do and what happens. No move triggers. No Edge is generated. This distinction matters: Edge comes from deliberate coordination, not from a follower going rogue.

### The Burden of Leadership

Followers are useful, but they are also a source of stress and complication.

**Followers get scared.** They may freeze when surprised, panic in the face of something overwhelming, or hesitate when the odds look bad. When this happens, ask the player what they do. If the PC gives the follower a direct order, the player can spend 1 Loyalty to have them overcome their fear. Otherwise, the GM decides.

**Followers won't always do as they're told.** They may resist if the order is unreasonable, foolish, or goes against their tags, instinct, or cost. If a follower won't comply, the PC needs to Sway them — or let it go.

**Play up a follower's instinct as a source of trouble.** If the follower always acts up, the player has no reason to Strengthen Their Bond. Let the instinct kick in often enough that Loyalty feels necessary.

### Followers in Fights

Followers are not the focus of a fight — the PCs are. Keep followers in the background unless they're directly relevant.

When a follower acts in a fight at the PC's direction, resolve it with Order Followers and one move at most. Zoom out and pick a single move to represent the off-screen action. Let everything else color the outcome.

Followers don't act independently in fights unless the GM makes a soft or hard move through them. If a follower is in the line of fire, show the danger and ask the player what they do.

### Followers at 0 Resilience

When a follower's last resilience circle fills, they are taken out. The GM picks one:
- They are dead
- They are incapacitated and need help
- They are out for now but will recover given time

It is not the PC's job to protect their follower. Followers are NPCs. **Let things burn.**

## Creating Followers

When writing a follower up, work through these steps:

1. **Name and descriptor** — who are they in a few words
2. **Tags** — 2–4, a mix of useful and problematic
3. **Resilience** — pick a track based on their toughness
4. **Armor** — only if they're the type to wear it
5. **Instinct** — one sentence, "to [do something]"
6. **Cost** — what keeps them following
7. **Description** — 2–3 sentences on who they are and how they act

---

## Updating and Losing Followers

Between sessions, review any followers the PCs have. If a tag or instinct no longer rings true, revise it. If the follower has grown or changed, add a new tag. If they've become a source of ongoing serious trouble, write them up as a threat instead.

Followers might stop being followers for many reasons — they die, they move on, the relationship breaks down, the fiction changes. If the split is messy, the former follower may become a threat. If they've been harboring resentment, they may betray the PC at a bad moment.
